    Kim Stanley Robinson is a literary science fiction writer from Davis, California. And while we never learn the name of the country where the story takes place, it bears many similarities to Imbolo Mbue‚Äôs home country of Cameroon, a relatively small West-Central African country that borders Nigeria to the north and Equatorial Guinea, Gabon, and the Republic of Congo to the south.

    Some people refer to Cameroon as ‚ÄúLittle Africa‚Äù because it encapsulates so many facets of this vast continent. And just like Cameroon, the story of Kosawa is a familiar one in Africa. Africa is an enormous continent, but many of its people have been the victims of violent Western European and American colonizers. The people of Kosawa, too, were often victimized by Western colonizers, such as slave traders, plantation owners, and oil company executives.

    Returning again to Cameroon, this land was historically inhabited by the Adamawa Emirate in the north and by various chiefdoms and fondoms in the south. That is, until the German Empire imposed brutal colonial rule in the late 1800s. The Germans controlled the colony they called Kamerun‚Äîafter the Portuguese word for shrimp‚Äîuntil the end of World War I in 1916.

    After the war, Cameroon was split in half between the English and the French. It wasn‚Äôt until the 1960s that Cameroon gained independence from England and France and reunified as one country. In the many years since, one political party has primarily controlled the country and has focused heavily on oil production to grow its economy. Meanwhile, the country has faced many problems, from political corruption to civil unrest to terrorism. In other words, many of the same problems facing the fictional country from How Beautiful We Were.

    Parallels between Kosawa and Ecuador

    Back to top ‚Üë

    But in terms of the plot of this novel, I‚Äôm reminded of another country, albeit in the Western Hemisphere. Ecuador is a small South American country on the Pacific coast, sharing a border with Peru to the south and Colombia to the north. The Amazon rainforest extends partially into Ecuador, and in the 1960s, the American oil company Texaco, since acquired by Chevron in 2001, started drilling for oil in the Ecuadorian rainforest in what is known as the Lago Agrio oil field. Literally translated: the sour lake oil field.

    You probably don‚Äôt need me to tell you the effects were disastrous. Texaco didn‚Äôt properly dispose of toxic waste from drilling operations‚Äîmeaning they dumped toxic wastewater into a river that provided drinking water to many indigenous people. Just like the residents of Kosawa in How Beautiful We Were, indigenous people in Ecuador started getting sick and dying. And just like the fictional American oil company Pexton from the novel, the very real oil company called Texaco‚Äînow a brand of California-based Chevron‚Äîdenied any wrongdoing at first, blaming the sickness and death among the indigenous people on poor sanitation. Which was, of course, a racist lie.

    Like the people of Kosawa, Ecuadorians finally had enough of this, and in 1993 some local villagers filed a massive class action lawsuit against Texaco. Seemingly against all odds, the plaintiffs won the class action suit, and in 2011, the Ecuadorian court ordered Chevron to pay $9.5 billion in damages. And sadly, much like the story of How Beautiful We Were, this story is still developing today. Even though Ecuador found Chevron responsible for oil drilling contamination in the Ecuadorian Amazon, Chevron has fought back against the ruling with everything it has.

    In 2018, Chevron won a very important appeals case with The Hague in which an international tribunal ruled that the 2011 ruling by Ecuador ‚Äú‚Ä¶was procured through fraud, bribery and corruption and was based on claims that had been already settled and released by the Republic of Ecuador years earlier.‚Äù This effectively rendered the 2011 ruling unenforceable, meaning that in the 25 years that passed since the first class action lawsuit was filed, the indigenous people from the region surrounding Lago Agrio still had not received reparations.

    In fact, three years after the 2018 tribunal ruling, the original plaintiffs still haven‚Äôt received reparations.     And finally, reminiscent of the fates that met Austin, the young journalist from B√©zam, and Thula, the quiet-village-girl-turned-fierce-activist, the American lawyer who represented the Ecuadorian villagers in the first class action lawsuit in 1993 has found himself in Chevron‚Äôs crosshairs.     I‚Äôll be honest: things don‚Äôt look good for Steven Donziger.     So what‚Äôs the takeaway from all of this?     And just like the battles against the fictional company Pexton from How Beautiful We Were, the court cases involving Chevron and Ecuador just go to show how far the fossil fuel industry will go to protect its interests.

    As the latest IPCC report makes abundantly clear, there is no scenario in which the fossil fuel industry continues to exist if we‚Äôre to have a livable planet. But how we take down the fossil fuel industry is the biggest question that remains unanswered.

    Violent versus nonviolent direct action

    Back to top ‚Üë

    As How Beautiful We Were starts to pick up steam, a clear dichotomy begins to emerge in how different villagers of Kosawa fight back against Pexton. We see this first when Malabo and his friends kidnap the men from Pexton, but this theme becomes more fleshed out after Thula leaves the village to attend university in the United States.

    After arriving in New York City, Thula reconnects with Austin, the journalist from Bezam who originally broke the story about Pexton‚Äôs misdeeds. Austin starts taking Thula to meetings with activists who work on various human rights issues, and it doesn‚Äôt take long before Thula becomes a fierce activist herself. But unlike some of the other villagers taking a stand against Pexton, Thula is staunchly committed to non-violent direct action.

    The first-person collective narrator known simply as The Children has a different approach, though. These people are Thula‚Äôs peers, and they exchange letters back and forth with her about what should be done to stop Pexton.     In fact, the character who seems to be the most well-off by the end is Juba, Thula‚Äôs younger brother who came back from the dead as a child after getting very sick from Pexton‚Äôs poison.     The best way forward?

    Back to top ‚Üë

    After reading How Beautiful We Were and reflecting on the world we currently live in, one that is honestly so much more bleak and dystopian than the one in this novel, I‚Äôm not sure of how we should proceed.

    High profile climate organizations like Extinction Rebellion and the Sunrise Movement are committed to peaceful direct action, but how long can this continue to be a viable strategy? At what point do some people renege on this principle and resort to violence? Many important movements throughout history have been fought with violence. Is that what the environmental movement will one day be forced to do?

    But honestly, out of the three responses I see in How Beautiful We Were, I think Juba‚Äôs response might be the most relatable to many people. Heartbroken and half-dead, many of us walk through life like zombies, complying with our evil capitalist overlords while we scheme to exploit the broken system to our advantage. Many of us know the system is fucked, but what can we do about it? It‚Äôs a vicious cycle until some external force breaks that cycle, or the house of cards comes crashing down under its own weight.

    I‚Äôm sure we‚Äôll see a combination of all three responses, as the environmental movement progresses and our insane society continues to flirt even more with the threat of apocalypse. I strongly believe there is no one right way to tackle this enormous problem. Perhaps a hybrid approach will be the path we take. As for me, I don‚Äôt want to see violence and bloodshed erupt as we struggle to save our planet. But I have to wonder: are there limits to peaceful protest, and what will happen if we cross that threshold?
